# PS-03: Accessible Campus Navigation for Students with Visual Impairments

> **Company:** Netrasemi
> **Vertical:** Smart Campus AI
> **Technology:** Edge AI Chip + Wearable Camera + Spatial Audio Output

---

## Problem Statement

India has 5+ million visually impaired individuals, and despite the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act (2016) requiring inclusive education, fewer than 2% of visually impaired students access higher education. One of the primary barriers is physical campus navigation — large, complex campuses with hundreds of buildings, multiple floors, changing layouts, construction zones, and dynamic obstacles (crowds, vehicles, new furniture) are genuinely dangerous and disorienting without visual cues.

Existing assistive navigation tools (white canes, guide dogs) provide basic obstacle avoidance but no contextual intelligence — they cannot tell a student they've arrived at the right building, that the elevator is 10 meters ahead, or that there's a wet floor hazard around the corner.

---

## Who Faces This Problem

- Visually impaired students enrolled in universities and colleges
- University accessibility officers responsible for RPWD Act compliance
- Disability rights organizations advocating for inclusive higher education
- Faculty who want to ensure visually impaired students can independently access all campus facilities

---

## Why the Problem Matters

- Campus independence is essential for educational dignity — students should not need constant escort
- RPWD Act compliance is a legal obligation for all educational institutions receiving government funding
- Visually impaired students who cannot navigate campus independently are effectively excluded from education
- Only 550 universities in India have registered Disability Resource Centers — and most lack navigation aids

---

## Proposed Solution

A wearable edge AI navigation assistant — Netrasemi chip embedded in a lightweight chest-worn device with a forward-facing camera and bone-conduction audio output (so ears remain free for ambient sound). The device provides real-time spatial audio guidance: describing the environment, identifying landmarks and signage, detecting and naming obstacles, and providing turn-by-turn navigation to any campus destination. All processing happens on the edge device; no camera feed leaves the student.

**Key features:**
- Real-time obstacle detection and spatial audio warning (< 100ms latency)
- Campus landmark and signage recognition (building names, room numbers, elevator buttons)
- Turn-by-turn indoor navigation using pre-mapped campus floor plan + real-time landmark anchoring
- Crowd density and safety warnings (busy corridor, stairs ahead, door opening toward you)
- NFC tap points at key campus locations for position confirmation
- Works without GPS in indoor environments
- 8-hour battery life; lightweight (<200g); water-resistant

---

## Why Edge AI Is Needed

- Processing delay > 100ms in navigation assistance causes the student to have already passed the relevant obstacle
- Camera feed from a wearable must never leave the device — the privacy stakes for a student are enormous
- Indoor environments have no GPS — navigation requires on-device spatial understanding
- The device must work in campus areas with congested WiFi — cloud dependency creates unreliable performance

---

## Desirability

Visually impaired students want independent navigation with dignity. University accessibility offices want to demonstrate RPWD Act compliance. Parents want confidence that their child can navigate campus safely. Disability rights NGOs want a product they can advocate for and distribute.

**Desirability Score: 9/10**

---

## Feasibility

Obstacle detection and object recognition for navigation assistance is well-studied (research prototype: Microsoft Seeing AI, Google Lookout). Indoor navigation without GPS using visual landmark recognition is an active research area. The wearable form factor and bone-conduction audio integration is achievable. Key challenge: building an India-specific campus landmark and signage recognition model, and the UX design for spatial audio navigation.

**Feasibility Score: 7/10**

## Possible MVP

A wearable device providing obstacle detection and 5 landmark recognition points (main gate, library, admin building, canteen, disability resource center) on one campus, tested with 5 visually impaired student volunteers over one semester. Track: navigation success rate, obstacle warning accuracy, student-reported confidence and independence.

**Timeline:** 14 weeks prototype, one semester (4 month) campus trial

## Risks and Challenges

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| User trust: wearable camera invasion of other students' privacy | High | Camera scope and privacy-preserving design; no image storage |
| Navigation failures in complex indoor environments | High | Graceful degradation: conservative guidance; request human help option |
| Battery life insufficient for full campus day | Medium | Lightweight battery optimization; charging station at DRC |
| Resistance from universities to invest in accessibility | High | RPWD Act enforcement leverage; government subsidy coverage |
