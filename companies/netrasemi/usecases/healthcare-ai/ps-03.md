# PS-03: Real-Time Surgical Instrument Tracking and Count Verification

> **Company:** Netrasemi
> **Vertical:** Healthcare AI
> **Technology:** Edge AI Vision Chip + Surgical Field Camera

---

## Problem Statement

Retained surgical instruments (RSIs) — surgical tools, sponges, or needles accidentally left inside a patient after surgery — cause severe complications, second surgeries, and deaths. The WHO estimates RSIs occur in 1 in every 5,000–7,000 surgeries globally. In India, where 10+ million surgeries are performed annually in public hospitals, manual instrument counting is the only safeguard — and it fails due to time pressure, fatigue, and distracted counting in busy operating theaters.

Current solutions (RFID tags on instruments, barcode scanning) are expensive, require infrastructure changes, and add workflow friction in already-stressed operating environments.

---

## Who Faces This Problem

- Surgeons and scrub nurses in government and private hospitals
- Hospital quality officers managing surgical safety compliance
- Patients undergoing any invasive surgical procedure
- Medical liability insurers

---

## Why the Problem Matters

- An RSI causes patient harm in 69% of cases and death in 6%
- Hospitals pay $100,000–$500,000 per RSI case in litigation and remediation
- WHO Surgical Safety Checklist mandates instrument counts — but manual counting has a documented failure rate
- India's National Accreditation Board for Hospitals (NABH) now requires surgical safety programs

---

## Proposed Solution

An edge AI camera unit mounted at the surgical field — integrated with the existing surgical lamp or as a standalone boom — powered by a Netrasemi chip. The system continuously tracks every instrument and sponge in the surgical field using computer vision, maintains a running count, and alerts the scrub nurse in real time if counts don't reconcile before closure. No RFID tags. No workflow changes. No internet connection.

**Key features:**
- Detects 200+ surgical instrument types and counts sponges
- Reconciles pre-op and post-op inventory automatically
- Alerts surgeon + scrub nurse when count discrepancy detected
- Records the surgical field video with instrument annotations for audit
- Works under surgical lamp lighting conditions (high glare, blood, fluid)

---

## Why Edge AI Is Needed

- Operating rooms cannot have network dependency — any connectivity failure is a patient safety failure
- Video processing of a surgical field (real-time, high resolution) is too bandwidth-intensive for cloud
- Surgical video is among the most sensitive healthcare data — it must stay within the hospital
- Latency of even 500ms for an instrument count alert is operationally unacceptable

---

## Desirability

Surgeons want to close without count anxiety. Scrub nurses want an automated backup to manual counting. Hospital administrators want to reduce RSI liability. Accreditation bodies want measurable surgical safety improvements. Patients simply want to not have a sponge left inside them.

**Desirability Score: 9/10**

## Feasibility

Object detection for surgical instruments is an active and well-published research area (CholecT50, M2CAI datasets). Netrasemi chips can run YOLO-class detection models in real time. The main challenges are: (1) occlusion handling (instruments overlapping, partially covered by surgeon's hands), (2) performance under surgical lighting glare, and (3) the long tail of uncommon instrument types.

**Feasibility Score: 7/10**

## Risks and Challenges

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Occlusion in cluttered surgical field | High | Multi-angle camera setup; temporal tracking models |
| Surgeon/nurse resistance to new OR equipment | Medium | Co-design with surgical teams; non-intrusive form factor |
| Regulatory pathway (Class C medical device) | High | Early CDSCO engagement; CE mark as parallel track |
| False alarms slowing surgical workflow | High | Tune for high specificity; alert only at count moments, not continuously |
