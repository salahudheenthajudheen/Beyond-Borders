# PS-02: Continuous Neonatal Monitoring for Low-Resource Hospital Settings

> **Company:** Netrasemi
> **Vertical:** Healthcare AI
> **Technology:** Edge AI Chip + Vision + Physiological Sensor Fusion

---

## Problem Statement

India has one of the world's highest neonatal mortality rates — 23 per 1,000 live births, accounting for 575,000 infant deaths annually. The majority occur in district and sub-district hospitals that have no neonatal ICUs (NICUs). High-risk newborns in these facilities are monitored intermittently by nurses — at best every 30 minutes — due to staff shortages. Critical deterioration events (apnea, sepsis onset, hypoxia) that take seconds to minutes to become fatal go undetected between manual checks.

Standard NICU monitoring equipment costs $50,000–$200,000 per bed — entirely out of reach for government district hospitals.

---

## Who Faces This Problem

- High-risk newborns in district and CHC hospitals without NICU capacity
- Nursing staff in under-resourced wards managing 8–15 neonates per nurse
- Pediatricians who cannot be at the bedside continuously
- Parents of high-risk newborns in public hospitals

---

## Why the Problem Matters

- 75% of neonatal deaths occur in the first week of life — most are preventable with monitoring
- The gap between high-resource and low-resource newborn outcomes is almost entirely a monitoring and response gap
- India has committed to reducing neonatal mortality to under 12/1,000 by 2030

---

## Proposed Solution

A low-cost (sub-$500) edge AI neonatal monitoring unit — bedside device with a Netrasemi chip — that continuously analyzes: (1) a non-contact thermal camera for breathing pattern and temperature, (2) a standard pulse oximeter clip for SpO2 and heart rate, and (3) an optional skin conductance sensor for early sepsis detection. The device runs a multi-signal anomaly detection model locally, alerting nurses instantly via bedside alarm and a central nursing station display when deterioration patterns are detected.

**Key features:**
- Continuous monitoring without nurse intervention
- Non-contact thermal breathing detection (no restraints on newborn)
- Apnea, bradycardia, and sepsis early-warning models
- Bedside alert + central ward display + SMS alert to on-call pediatrician
- Battery backup for power outages (critical in district hospitals)
- Works offline; logs all events for clinical audit

---

## Why Edge AI Is Needed

- Hospital wards may have no or unreliable internet
- Alert latency must be < 1 second — cloud round-trip is unacceptable for neonatal emergencies
- Patient physiological data is extremely sensitive — cannot be transmitted to cloud without consent frameworks
- Low-power edge chip enables 8+ hours battery backup on a small form factor device

---

## Desirability

Nurses want fewer missed deterioration events. Pediatricians want real-time alerting. Hospital administrators want to improve outcomes without hiring more nursing staff. Parents want their baby monitored continuously. Government health programs want NICU-level outcomes in district hospitals.

**Desirability Score: 10/10**

---

## Feasibility

Non-contact respiration monitoring via thermal imaging is an active research area with validated results. Pulse oximetry integration is trivial. The fusion model for apnea/bradycardia detection is well-studied. The primary challenges are: (1) clinical validation, (2) CDSCO certification as a medical device, and (3) the thermal camera resolution requirements.

**Feasibility Score: 7/10**

---

## Viability

**Revenue models:**
- Government hospital procurement via NHM capital expenditure budgets (~₹30,000 per unit target)
- Rental model for private nursing homes (₹2,000–3,000/month)
- Outcome-based contracting with district health authorities (payment per avoided neonatal death)
- Export to high-burden countries (Bangladesh, Nigeria, Pakistan, Ethiopia)

**Viability Score: 8/10**

## Possible MVP

An edge monitoring unit tracking SpO2 + thermal respiration for 10 neonates across 2 district hospitals over 3 months. Track: alert accuracy, nurse response time improvement, adverse event rate vs. control ward.

**Timeline:** 14 weeks prototype, 6 months clinical pilot

---

## Risks and Challenges

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| High false alarm rate (alarm fatigue) | High | Prioritize specificity in model design; tiered alert levels |
| CDSCO Class C medical device regulation | High | Engage regulatory consultant; multi-year pathway |
| Power reliability in district hospitals | High | Integrated UPS; battery backup minimum 8 hours |
| Model failure in edge cases (twins, preterm morphology) | Medium | Extensive clinical dataset; human override always primary |
