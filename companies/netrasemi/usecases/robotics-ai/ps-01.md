# PS-01: Autonomous Last-Mile Delivery Robot for Dense Urban Neighborhoods

> **Company:** Netrasemi
> **Vertical:** Robotics AI
> **Technology:** Edge AI Chip + LiDAR + Multi-Camera + Actuator Control

---

## Problem Statement

India's e-commerce sector is projected to reach $200B by 2027, but last-mile delivery — the final kilometer from fulfillment hub to doorstep — accounts for 40–55% of total logistics cost and is the most operationally chaotic part of the supply chain. Delivery density in urban neighborhoods enables economical delivery when riders are available, but 30–40% of deliveries require multiple attempts due to missed delivery windows, and rider attrition and cost inflation are squeezing margins for every logistics operator.

Autonomous last-mile robots — common in US and European campuses and neighborhoods — have not penetrated India because existing platforms are designed for wide, smooth sidewalks in regulated environments. India's footpaths are narrow, uneven, occupied by street vendors, motorcycles, and animals, and entirely unpredictable.

---

## Who Faces This Problem

- E-commerce logistics companies (Delhivery, Ekart, Xpressbees)
- Quick commerce operators (Zepto, Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart) needing 10-minute delivery
- Hyperlocal delivery startups
- Delivery riders facing dangerous traffic, poor working conditions, and unstable income
- Urban residents frustrated by missed delivery windows

---

## Why the Problem Matters

- Last-mile logistics is India's largest unresolved cost problem in e-commerce
- Delivery rider gig work is increasingly unsustainable economically and physically
- Quick commerce 10-minute delivery promises are impossible to fulfill at scale with human riders
- Carbon emissions from delivery motorcycles are a growing urban air quality problem

---

## Proposed Solution

A sidewalk delivery robot designed specifically for Indian urban conditions — Netrasemi chip + 3D LiDAR + stereo camera array + thermal camera — that navigates dense footpaths at walking speed, avoiding pedestrians, obstacles, animals, and road hazards autonomously. The robot operates a 2km delivery radius from a local hub, carries 15–20kg of cargo, and integrates with existing delivery management systems. When the Netrasemi edge AI detects an environment it cannot handle (extreme crowd density, construction zone), it requests remote human assistance via its 4G link — but navigates 95%+ of its route without intervention.

**Key features:**
- 360° perception: LiDAR + stereo vision + thermal (for pedestrian detection at night)
- India-specific training: handles unpaved surfaces, open drains, street vendor obstacles
- On-device path planning: no cloud needed for real-time navigation
- OTP-based contactless delivery: recipient unlocks cargo compartment via SMS code
- < 3km/h safe operating speed on footpaths
- 6-hour operating range on single charge; swappable battery in hub

---

## Why Edge AI Is Needed

- Navigation decisions must be made in milliseconds — cloud latency causes collision
- LiDAR + camera data generates 50–100MB/s — impossible to transmit to cloud for processing
- Connectivity is intermittent in dense urban environments (network congestion, building interference)
- Real-time object detection and path planning on a mobile platform requires dedicated edge AI silicon

---

## Desirability

Logistics companies want to reduce last-mile cost per delivery from ₹50–80 to ₹20–30. Quick commerce operators need to fulfill 10-minute promises without infinite rider headcount. Consumers want reliable delivery without waiting at home. Riders want to move to higher-value tasks.

**Desirability Score: 8/10**

---

## Feasibility

Autonomous navigation in indoor and campus environments is mature. India-condition outdoor navigation is genuinely hard — the long tail of edge cases (temple processions, flooded footpaths, aggressive dogs) is enormous. Netrasemi's edge AI provides the compute backbone; the real challenge is the 3–5 years of training data collection and model refinement required to achieve safe operation in Indian conditions.

**Feasibility Score: 6/10**

---

## Viability

**Revenue models:**
- Robot-as-a-service: ₹20–30 per delivery to logistics companies (vs. ₹50–80 for human delivery)
- Fleet ownership: sell robots to logistics hubs, provide software subscription
- Data product: anonymized urban movement and obstacle data valuable to city planners
- Government smart city program integration

**Viability Score: 7/10**

## Possible MVP

A teleoperated-first prototype (human remote control with AI assistance) navigating a 500m mapped route in one residential neighborhood, delivering 10 orders per day over 30 days. Track: delivery success rate, intervention frequency, user acceptance.

**Timeline:** 20 weeks prototype, 3 months teleoperated pilot

## Risks and Challenges

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Theft or vandalism of robot | High | GPS tracking; locked cargo; community familiarity program |
| Robot-pedestrian collision causing injury | Medium | Speed limiting (< 3km/h); force-limited actuators; conservative safety model |
| Regulatory approval for pavement robots | High | Engage MoRTH; pilot in gated communities first |
| Extremely long tail of India-specific edge cases | Very High | Teleoperation fallback; continuous learning architecture |
